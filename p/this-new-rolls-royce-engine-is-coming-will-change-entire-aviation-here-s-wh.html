In fact, Rolls-Royce grew from a combat engine manufacturer into a ruler of the commercial skies, but that journey was far from simple. The company made its name in the First World War, when its Eagle engine dominated British bombers and transport aircraft. Later, it secured its place in history with the legendary Merlin—the heart of the Supermarine Spitfire and the Hawker Hurricane—that played a decisive role in the Battle of Britain. Yet, when the war ended, Rolls-Royce was forced to change its business model. The company quickly adapted to the jet age, developing the Derwent engine for the Gloster Meteor—Britain’s first jet aircraft—and especially the Nene, one of the most widely exported jet engines in the world, which was fitted on both American and Soviet fighters.

In the 19 sixties and seventies, Rolls-Royce continued its revolution in commercial aviation with the RB 2 1 1. Although the company faced a financial crisis, the RB 2 1 1 became a historic turning point by introducing, for the first time, the high-bypass turbofan—a technology that has since become the gold standard of modern aviation. However, its greatest challenge lay in optimizing the rotational speeds of the inner sections. Every component, from the compressors to the turbines, had its own operating requirements, and even the slightest mismatch could reduce efficiency, burn more fuel, and generate deafening noise.
